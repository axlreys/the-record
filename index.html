REPUBLIC OF THE PHILIPPINES
ARMED FORCES OF THE PHILIPPINES · GENERAL COURT-MARTIAL No. 2
People of the Philippines  versus  Cdt 1CL Axl Rey L. Sanopao

Convicted without direct evidence.

A documented account of the court-martial of Cadet Axl Rey L. Sanopao, assembled entirely from the record.

On 31 March 2025, a general court-martial found Cadet Sanopao guilty of instigating the maltreatment that led to the death of a fourth-class cadet, Darwin D. Dormitorio. In the same decision, the court admitted more than once that no direct evidence and no eyewitness ever placed such an order in his mouth. Every fact on this page is drawn from the court's own decision, the sworn transcripts, the charge sheet, or the civilian prosecutor's resolutions. Nothing here is added; the citations are shown so any reader can check them.

01

What he was actually charged with

The Amended Charge Sheet accused Cadet Sanopao of a single act under Article of War 97 (Conduct Prejudicial to Good Order and Military Discipline): that on 17 September 2019 he did "instigate/order the physical maltreatment" committed by Cadet Shalimar Imperial Jr. and Cadet Felix Lumbag Jr. against Cadet Darwin Dormitorio, "resulting to and contributing to the death of the latter."

He was not charged with murder, homicide, or torture. He was not accused of laying a hand on anyone. The entire case rested on one theory: that he gave an order. And at pre-trial, the prosecution formally stipulated on the record that it would not prove conspiracy. In law, that means only his own act could be charged to him, not the acts of the others or the death itself.

Source: Amended Charge Sheet; Pre-Trial Conference, TSN 5 February 2021, p. 7; Pre-Trial Order, 5 March 2021.

02

What the record shows happened

The one act attributed to Cadet Sanopao was in the morning. Everything the record describes as violence happened afterward, in the evening, and was inflicted by other people, after the hospital had examined Dormitorio and sent him back cleared.

  • 17 Sep
    ~0652H

    The combat boots

    Cadet Sanopao called Cadet Dormitorio to retrieve a pair of combat boots he had entrusted to him. When the boots could not be found, he told Dormitorio to have his two assigned "buddies," Cadet Imperial and Cadet Lumbag, help look for them. This is the sole act the prosecution calls an "order."

  • 17 Sep
    ~0930H

    Brought to the PMA Station Hospital

    Dormitorio was admitted and observed for roughly seven hours by the attending physician, Capt. Flor Apple Apostol, and the duty nurse, 2Lt. Cathrynne Ruzzelle Caterial-Bulatao. He was assessed for a urinary tract infection; his whole body was seen.

  • 17 Sep
    ~1600–1700H

    Discharged and cleared

    He was discharged with normal vital signs and no fresh injury, only an old, healing bruise from an earlier consultation. He was cleared to return to barracks. Cadet Sanopao had no further contact with him.

  • 17 Sep
    evening

    Further assaults by others

    After discharge, the record describes Dormitorio being assaulted again by other cadets through the evening, and his condition worsening overnight.

  • 18 Sep
    ~0100–0515H

    Death

    Dormitorio collapsed and died in the early morning hours and was pronounced dead on arrival at the hospital. The cause of death was blunt abdominal trauma with massive internal bleeding.

03

The court admitted there was no direct evidence

The conviction is remarkable for what the tribunal conceded in its own decision. In its written findings, the court acknowledged, more than once, that nothing directly tied Cadet Sanopao to any order to harm.

"There were no eyewitnesses … that he indeed gave orders or instructions to his co-accused to physically maltreat Cadet Dormitorio."
GCM No. 2 Joint Decision, p. 72
"It is very clear from the amount of evidence presented by the prosecution that no direct evidence, testimonial or otherwise, linked Cadet Sanopao to the physical assault that ultimately led to the eventual death of the late Cadet Dormitorio."
GCM No. 2 Joint Decision, p. 75
04

No witness heard an order

This was not a gap the court had to infer. Witness after witness, including Dormitorio's own roommates and the two co-accused, testified under oath that they never heard Cadet Sanopao order anyone to be harmed. The exchanges below are quoted from the transcripts.

Capt. Jose Carlos Gamos, investigating officerTSN · 19 Mar 2021 · p. 7
Q.

You're certain that there was no direct or express order from Cadet Sanopao for Cadet Imperial and Cadet Lumbag to maltreat Cadet Dormitorio?

A.

Yes, Your Honor.

Cadet Jason Sierra, Dormitorio's roommateTSN · 26 Nov 2021 · p. 62
Q.

So you did not hear the order of Cadet Sanopao on Cadet Dormitorio. Correct?

A.

Yes, your Honor.

Cadet Ivan Jeff Dela Cruz, Dormitorio's roommateTSN · 4 Mar 2022 · p. 45
Q.

And from that room, you did not hear Cadet Sanopao give an order of whatever sort. Is that correct?

A.

The only thing I heard was Dormitorio murmuring, "saan na ba yun?" ("where is it?").

Cadet Shalimar Imperial Jr., co-accusedTSN · 12 May 2023 · p. 28
Q.

Did he tell you to order Cadet Dormitorio to do some exercises?

A.

No, Your Honor.

Q.

So it's just your initiative?

A.

Yes, Your Honor. As a buddy, we tend to correct some mistakes, Your Honor.

Cadet Felix Lumbag Jr., co-accusedTSN · 12 May 2023 · p. 34
Q.

Did he have any involvement in the death of Cadet Dormitorio, or none?

A.

None, Your Honor. Cadet Sanopao did not order us to cause the death of Cadet Dormitorio, Your Honor.

And the prosecution conceded it at summation

Asked directly whether any words of command ever came from Cadet Sanopao, the Trial Judge Advocate answered: "there is no categorical statement from the witnesses that they heard or saw Cadet Sanopao instruct both Cadet Lumbag and Cadet Imperial to do anything to Cadet Dormitorio."

Oral Summation, TSN 18 August 2023.

05

The only instruction proven was to find the boots

Two of Cadet Sanopao's own roommates, Cadet Buenafred Salvador Jr. and Cadet Mikael Burong, were in the room and within hearing distance during the entire interaction. Both swore that the sole instruction was to help look for the missing boots. Their affidavits were offered in evidence, then excluded by the court.

"I know for sure that Sanopao did not order the buddies to maltreat or cause any physical harm to Dormitorio … not once did I hear or see Sanopao maltreat or cause to maltreat Dormitorio."
Affidavit of Cadet Buenafred Salvador Jr., Sanopao's roommate, 7 November 2019 (offered as Excluded Evidence)

Even the prosecution ultimately agreed, on the record, that whatever order was given pertained only to the boots:

Colloquy: Law Member & Trial Judge AdvocateTSN · 26 May 2023 · p. 101
Q.

As long as it pertains to the finding of the combat boots?

A.

Yes, your Honors.

06

The medicine places the fatal injury after he was gone

If the morning interaction caused nothing physical, it cannot have contributed to the death. The medical witnesses, including the prosecution's own and the court's own, established exactly that.

Capt. Flor Apple Apostol, attending physicianTSN · 15 Jul 2021 · pp. 19–20
Q.

You are very sure, doctor, that there was no indication of hematoma or contusions … from the admission in the morning up until 1700H of September 17, 2019?

A.

During the morning of September 17, what we noticed is the old contusion hematoma … previously seen during September 6, 2019.

Q.

You're very sure about that, doctor?

A.

Yes, Your Honor.

2Lt. Cathrynne Caterial-Bulatao, duty nurseTSN · 26 May 2023 · p. 16
Q.

Would you confirm that you did not see marks of possible injury on Dormitorio?

A.

If you're talking about fresh injury, wala po akong nakita (I saw none). But the next dawn, when we checked the body, it was very different. I saw a lot of markings, purplish in color.

Dr. Vladimir Villaseñor, defense forensic pathologistTSN · 15 Jun 2023 · pp. 11–14
Q.

So, conclusively, the injuries found by Dr. Bandonill were not present during the time the patient Dormitorio was observed and monitored on September 17, from 9:30 AM up to his discharge at 4:30 PM?

A.

Seven hours, Sir, in the hospital: if there are no signs of shock, there are no injuries, no vital organs injured, or even blood vessels lacerated at the time the patient was observed from 9 AM to 4 PM, Sir.

Q.

With all those ruptured internal organs, would the patient be ambulatory? Meaning, can he walk on his own at all?

A.

No, Sir. The patient will collapse, Sir. And lose consciousness.

Q.

Then when was the fatal injury inflicted, and when did death occur?

A.

The injury was inflicted within about two hours of death. The victim might have died already in the early morning, between 12 and 2 o'clock in the morning of September 18.

Dr. Ronald Bandonill, NBI medico-legal, the court's own witnessTSN · 2 Jun 2023 · pp. 16–17
Q.

So the massive injuries that mostly caused his death were inflicted sometime between the afternoon and the early morning?

A.

From the time he was discharged from the hospital until he was brought to the hospital again the next day, that would have been the time the massive injury was inflicted, Sir. Estimated time of death, around 1 to 2 AM.

07

A civilian prosecutor dismissed the same facts

The Office of the City Prosecutor of Baguio City reviewed the very same witnesses and documents. Applying probable cause (the lowest threshold in our law), it dismissed all charges against Cadet Sanopao for lack of probable cause on 5 June 2020, and affirmed that dismissal when the complainant sought reconsideration.

"Justice for a fallen cadet does not mean an indictment that is free-for-all. The law may be harsh, but it is the law."
Joint Review Resolution, Office of the City Prosecutor of Baguio City, 28 September 2020

When a separate panel of prosecutors later re-examined the record on order of the Regional Trial Court, it charged only Cadet Imperial and Cadet Lumbag. Cadet Sanopao was charged in neither. Two independent civilian reviews thus looked at the same evidence and found no basis to charge him at all.

Supplemental Resolution, Special Panel of Prosecutors (per RTC Branch V, Baguio City), 12 October 2020.

08

Convicted anyway, and sentenced as a killer

Despite its own admissions, the court found Cadet Sanopao guilty of Article of War 97 (that he "instigated or ordered" the maltreatment) and declared the conduct "tantamount to homicide by acts of torture." It imposed Reclusion Temporal, 14 years 8 months 1 day to 20 years, plus dismissal from the service: the same homicide-grade penalty given to the two cadets who physically beat Dormitorio.

He was punished for homicide and torture, offenses the charge sheet never alleged and which, the court itself acknowledged, were never pleaded against him.

GCM No. 2 Joint Decision, pp. 92–93.

09

The injustice: the law the court set aside

A conviction is not only a question of evidence; it is a question of law. On this record the court departed from the Constitution, the Manual for Courts-Martial, the Articles of War, and settled Supreme Court doctrine at nearly every turn. Each departure below is stated with what happened, the specific authority it violated, and what that law actually requires.

1 · Punished for a crime that was never charged

The charge was only Article of War 97: instigating maltreatment. Yet the dispositive branded the conduct "homicide by acts of torture" and used those unpleaded acts of "torture" to push the penalty to its maximum, even after the court itself held that torture had never been alleged against him.

Violated1987 Constitution, Art. III, Sec. 14(1)–(2)R.A. 9745, Sec. 22People v. Natimdim, G.R. 201867 (2020)

The lawAn accused has the constitutional right to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation against him. Any fact that raises the penalty must itself be alleged in the charge. A person cannot be sentenced for an offense (or an aggravating circumstance) that was never charged and that he was never given the chance to meet.

2 · A military court imposed a civilian penalty it had no power to impose

The court recharacterized the offense as homicide under Article 249 of the Revised Penal Code and imposed Reclusion Temporal, a civilian penalty. That merged a military disciplinary charge with civilian penal statutes.

ViolatedR.A. 7055, Sec. 1MCM, Secs. 7, 11, 12Gonzales v. Abaya, G.R. 164007 (2006)Navales v. Abaya, G.R. 162318 (2004)

The lawUnder R.A. 7055, when a member of the AFP commits an act penalized under the Revised Penal Code, jurisdiction belongs to the civil courts, not a court-martial. A court-martial's power reaches only offenses under the Articles of War. As Gonzales v. Abaya holds, a service member may be tried under the Articles of War only independently of, and separately from, any charge for the same act penalized under the Revised Penal Code.

3 · The acts of others were blamed on him, though conspiracy was abandoned

Having stipulated it would not prove conspiracy, the prosecution could charge Cadet Sanopao only with his own act. Yet the death and the blows struck by others were laid at his door, and he drew the same penalty as the cadets who inflicted them.

Prosecution stipulation at pre-trialTSN · 5 Feb 2021 · p. 7

On the record, the prosecution manifested that it "does not intend to prove the existence of conspiracy among CDTs Sanopao, Lumbag and Imperial." The concession was carried into the Pre-Trial Order dated 5 March 2021. In law, that means only Cadet Sanopao's own act may be charged to him, never the acts of the others or the death itself.

ViolatedRevised Penal Code, Arts. 8 & 17(2)People v. Yanson-Dumancas, G.R. 133527-28 (1999)People v. Hirang, 803 Phil. 277 (2017)

The lawWithout conspiracy, the maxim "the act of one is the act of all" does not apply: each accused answers only for his own act. To convict a "principal by inducement," the inducement must be the determining cause of the crime, so direct and powerful as to amount to a command. Asking a plebe to find missing boots is not such an inducement, and the executors themselves testified the correction was their own initiative.

4 · Convicted on an opinion the court had already excluded, from a witness who was never an expert

The only thing linking Cadet Sanopao to an "order" was the pre-trial investigating officer's personal opinion that telling a plebe to find his boots was "encouraging maltreatment," an opinion he grounded expressly in his own memories as a former cadet. But Lt. Col. Kalang-ad was never qualified as an expert. At the offer of his testimony the defense objected that he "has no personal knowledge of the events." When the prosecution then tried to have his cadet-experience opinions treated as those of an expert resource person, Cadet Sanopao's counsel expressly refused, and the court agreed to treat him only as an ordinary witness, then barred any further questions about his personal experience as a cadet.

Defense counsel & Law Member: qualifying the witnessTSN · 30 Jun 2021 · pp. 24–25

Atty. Santiago (defense). "We will not treat him as an expert witness per se … we will make it on record that we are only treating Lt. Col. Kalang-ad as an ordinary witness, Your Honor."

Law Member. "In that case, from now on, the Court will not allow questions which will deal as to the personal experience of the witness in the academy when he was a cadet."

His opinion was therefore incompetent, was objected to, and was excluded at trial. Yet the convicting decision revived that very excluded opinion (that an "endorsement" meant an intent to maltreat) and made it the whole basis of the conviction.

ViolatedMCM, Sec. 78 (proof beyond reasonable doubt)Rules of Court, Rule 130 (opinion evidence)The court's own exclusion ruling, TSN 30 Jun 2021, pp. 24–25

The lawOnly a qualified expert may give opinion testimony; an ordinary witness's opinion is not competent proof of another person's intent. Once the court itself ruled Lt. Col. Kalang-ad an ordinary witness and shut out his experience-based opinions, that evidence was gone, and evidence a court has excluded cannot be revived to convict. Guilt must rest on legal and competent evidence beyond reasonable doubt.

5 · The exculpatory eyewitnesses were shut out

The affidavits of Cadet Salvador and Cadet Burong, the two roommates who actually heard the instruction, were declared "irrelevant" and excluded, after the prosecution dismissed the very tenor of the only alleged order as irrelevant.

Law Member & Trial Judge Advocate, on the defense's percipient witnessesTSN · 26 May 2023 · p. 101

Law Member. "So can you not stipulate if the tenor is just whether it's, 'you find my combat boots,' or 'please find my combat boots'?"

Trial Judge Advocate. "For the prosecution, your Honors, this would be irrelevant. We are willing to stipulate that an order was given by Cadet Sanopao, as long as it pertains to the finding of the combat boots."

On that footing the two eyewitnesses were excused. Their affidavits were formally tendered as excluded evidence at the promulgation hearing on 23 June 2023, for the express purpose of proving the true tenor of the order and ruling out any claim that Cadet Sanopao ordered maltreatment.

Violated1987 Constitution, Art. III, Sec. 14MCM, Sec. 49(F) & (G)ICCPR, Art. 14(3)(e)

The lawDue process guarantees the accused a genuine opportunity to present his defense and his witnesses; the Manual entitles both sides to present and support their contentions. Excluding the only witnesses to the actual words of the alleged order removed the defense from the decisive issue in the case.

6 · The court filled the gap with "judicial notice" of its own cadet experience

Confronted with its own admission of "no direct evidence," the panel did not acquit. In its written decision, at page 78, it declared that it "applied the judicial notice rule in appreciating the testimony of Lt. Col. Kalang-ad, as more than the majority of its members are themselves graduates of the Philippine Military Academy and had undergone the same ordeal." It thus supplied the decisive inference, that an "endorsement" meant an intent to maltreat, from the judges' own memories of being hazed.

ViolatedJoint Decision, p. 78MCM, Sec. 125Rules of Court, Rule 129, Sec. 2State Prosecutors v. Judge Muro, A.M. RTJ-92-876 (1994)

The lawJudicial notice lets a court accept, without proof, only facts that are indisputable and commonly known: geography, the calendar, official acts. Section 125 lists exactly what a court-martial may notice; internal cadet customs and a defendant's intent are not on it. A disputed fact at the heart of the case can never be "noticed," and a judge may not decide on facts he knows only personally. That is precisely what the panel did.

7 · The court called its own medico-legal witnesses without the defense

On 19 May 2023 the panel called the PNP medico-legal officer, Police Maj. Nolan Genova, as "a witness for the court," and on 2 June 2023 it did the same with the NBI's Dr. Ronald Bandonill. These were not minor or character witnesses. They were the court's own witnesses, testifying to the two decisive facts of the whole case: the cause of Cadet Dormitorio's death and when the fatal injury was inflicted. Cadet Sanopao's counsel of choice, Atty. Ma. Concepcion Santiago, was neither notified nor present; the morning session was closed before she returned from the lunch recess. Neither witness was cross-examined by the defense at all.

Who asked the questions

Of at least 181 questions put to Police Maj. Genova, the panel asked 176 (about 97%), the prosecutor 5, and the defense none (0%). Of the 76 questions put to Dr. Bandonill, the panel asked 70 (about 92%), the prosecutor 6, and the defense none (0%). The panel did not preside over an examination. It conducted one.

ViolatedMCM, Secs. 45, 49 & 75 (Sec. 75-C: the defense conducts cross of court witnesses)1987 Constitution, Art. III, Sec. 14(2)Tabuena v. Sandiganbayan, G.R. 103501-03 (1997)People v. Opida, G.R. L-46272 (1986)ICCPR, Art. 14(3)(e)

The lawA witness for the court is still a witness who must be confronted. Section 75-C guarantees, without qualification, that defense counsel conducts the cross-examination of witnesses for the court, and the accused keeps his right to counsel of his choice. This matters most, not least, when the witness is testifying to the facts that decide the case, the cause and timing of death. When the judges ask about 97% of the questions and the defense asks none, the tribunal has "descended into the arena" and lost "the cold neutrality of an impartial judge" (Tabuena; Opida).

8 · The presiding officer sat in judgment of his own institution

When the decision was promulgated, the President of the court-martial, Brig. Gen. Paulo Teodoro, simultaneously served as Assistant Superintendent of the Philippine Military Academy, the very institution whose culture, command, and hazing practices the panel set out to judge.

ViolatedMCM, Sec. 58(e)Dacera v. Judge Dizon, A.M. RTJ-00-1573

The lawA judge must have no personal or institutional interest in the outcome; litigants are entitled to "the cold neutrality of an impartial judge." Presiding over a case whose evidentiary foundation is one's own institution is a disqualifying conflict. The appearance of impartiality is destroyed even where actual bias is not proved.

9 · Pre-trial rights were denied: a 24-hour "hasty investigation"

The probe was described on the record as a "mabilisang imbestigasyon" (a hasty investigation), completed within 24 hours because the death had become a national issue. On the stand, the officers who conducted it admitted what that speed cost.

Lt. Col. Manuel Kalang-ad, pre-trial investigating officerTSN · 5 Feb 2021 · pp. 47–48
Q.

Did you allow each of the accused to cross-examine available witnesses?

A.

None.

Q.

Did you allow them to present witnesses on their behalf to explain their side?

A.

No, Your Honor.

Maj. Jeffrey Batistiana, company tactical officerTSN · 19 Nov 2021 · pp. 50–51
Q.

You also did not furnish them a copy of the delinquency report, correct? And you did not give the 24-hour period for them to explain?

A.

Yes, Your Honor.

Q.

And they were never informed of your recommendation, correct?

A.

Yes, Your Honor.

ViolatedArticle of War 71MCM, Secs. 30(c) & 35(a)Cudia v. Superintendent, PMA, G.R. 211362 (2015)

The lawBefore a charge may be referred for trial, Article of War 71 requires a thorough and impartial investigation in which the accused is given full opportunity to cross-examine witnesses and present a defense. As the Supreme Court held in Cudia, "the PMA is not immune from the strictures of due process."

10 · The burden of proof was turned upside down

During the joint trial on 12 May 2023, a panel member, Col. Resurreccion, pressed a co-accused, the cadet the prosecution's own witnesses placed as having kicked Dormitorio that evening, to prove his own innocence:

Panel member (Col. Resurreccion), to a co-accusedTSN · 12 May 2023 · p. 92

Panel member. "Papaano mo kami macoconvince without reasonable doubt na hindi ikaw yung naka last touch sa ulo ni Dormitorio?" (How will you convince us, beyond reasonable doubt, that you were not the last to touch Dormitorio's head?)

The demand was addressed to a co-accused, not to Cadet Sanopao. But in a single joint trial before the same panel, it laid bare the standard the tribunal applied to all the accused: prove your innocence, rather than let the prosecution prove guilt.

Violated1987 Constitution, Art. III, Sec. 14(2)MCM, Sec. 78People v. Jaen, G.R. 241946 (2019)ICCPR, Art. 14(2)

The lawEvery accused is presumed innocent, and the burden of proving guilt beyond reasonable doubt rests on the prosecution alone. It never shifts. To demand that the accused prove his own innocence inverts the most basic guarantee of criminal justice. Where the evidence allows an innocent reading, the accused must be acquitted.

11 · The panel coached the prosecutor against a defense witness

On 15 September 2023, while a defense witness, Lt. Col. Ramon Gualberto, was testifying about the loopholes in the investigation, a panel member turned to the Trial Judge Advocate, the prosecutor, and fed him the objection to raise against the defense's own witness:

Panel member & Trial Judge AdvocateTSN · 15 Sep 2023

Panel member. "Ah TJA, anong tawag dun? Pagka hindi nag-sign ng sworn affidavit?" (What do you call that? When one does not sign a sworn affidavit?)

Trial Judge Advocate. "Hearsay din siya, sir." (It's hearsay too, sir.)

ViolatedMCM, Sec. 41(c) & (d)Dacera v. Judge Dizon, A.M. RTJ-00-1573People v. Opida, G.R. L-46272 (1986)

The lawThe Trial Judge Advocate is the prosecutor; the panel is the judge. A tribunal that hands the prosecution its objections against a defense witness has stopped being a neutral adjudicator. Section 41(d) forbids any act inconsistent with a genuine desire to have the whole truth revealed, and the judges may not do the prosecutor's work for him. Litigants are entitled to "the cold neutrality of an impartial judge."

10

Where the case stands

Cadet Sanopao has been in continuous detention since 18 September 2019, the day Dormitorio died, now approaching seven years. His pay and allowances have been withheld since April 2025. His appeal to the Chief of Staff, Armed Forces of the Philippines, as reviewing authority, is pending.

He does not ask anyone to forget Cadet Dormitorio. He asks only that the record be read as it is written, and that a man not be punished as a killer for a death the evidence places in other hands, at another hour.
